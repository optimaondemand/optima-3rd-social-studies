Word Roots
Latin Root: memor- — "mindful, remembering"

The Latin root memor- means "mindful" or "remembering." A patriotic holiday is partly a memor- day: a day a whole country decides to be mindful of something together. The root shows up all over the place once you know to look.

📖

memory — something you keep in mind · memorial — a thing or day that helps people remember · memorable — worth remembering · remember — to bring something back into your mind

🔎 Discovery Fact
A memoir is a true book a person writes about their own life — literally, "a thing made of memory." Same Latin root, written down.

Learn the Story
📖
A Year of Remembering
Seven holidays, two big ideas

A patriotic holiday is a day a country chooses, on purpose, to stop and pay attention to something important about itself. Some are loud and bright. Some are quiet and serious. Both are needed. Here's the U.S. timeline, in order, with what each holiday honors.

🕊️ Martin Luther King Jr. Day — third Monday of January. Honors Dr. King, a peaceful leader who worked for fair treatment of all Americans. People often serve their community on this day in his memory.

🇺🇸 Presidents' Day — third Monday of February. Honors George Washington (our first President) and other U.S. Presidents. Originally for Washington's birthday on February 22.

🕯️ Memorial Day — last Monday of May. A quiet holiday. It honors the men and women who died serving in the U.S. military. Many families visit cemeteries and place flags or wreaths on graves.

🚩 Flag Day — June 14. Honors the American flag itself, which was adopted on this day in 1777. People fly flags on their houses.

🎆 Independence Day — July 4. The big one. Celebrates the day in 1776 when the Declaration of Independence said the United States was a free country. Parades, fireworks, picnics.

📜 Constitution Day — September 17. Honors the day in 1787 when the Constitution — the document you opened in Lesson 2 — was signed. Schools across the country teach about it.

🎖️ Veterans Day — November 11. Honors all the men and women who have served in the U.S. military, whether they came home or not. Different from Memorial Day, which is just for those who died.

If you look at all seven, you'll notice they split into two kinds. Celebrating holidays are bright — fireworks, flags, parades. They say "Isn't this country great?" Remembering holidays are quieter — wreaths, ceremonies, moments of silence. They say "Don't forget what it cost."

🤔

Pause & Think: Think about one patriotic holiday your family marks in some way. What does your family do that day? Does it feel more like celebrating or more like remembering?

Council Chamber
If a community had to give the most attention to one kind of patriotic holiday — the bright "celebrating" kind (like Independence Day) or the quiet "remembering" kind (like Memorial Day) — which one matters more, and why?
🎆 Side A: Celebrating

Bright holidays remind us we're lucky to live in a free country. Fireworks, parades, and flags pull whole communities outside together. Kids grow up loving their country because of those days. If we don't celebrate, the freedoms we have start to feel ordinary.

🕯️ Side B: Remembering

Quiet holidays remind us our freedoms were not free — somebody paid for them. Wreaths, silent moments, and graveyard flags teach us to take service seriously. A country that only celebrates and never remembers can forget what its holidays are about.

Honest curators will admit both sides have something. Most countries do both — and the United States is no exception. But in this Council Chamber, you'll pick one and explain why. In Tab 4, you'll write your recommendation with two reasons and one piece of evidence from today's lesson.

⏳

Then & Now: Memorial Day started right after the Civil War, in the 1860s, when families went to small cemeteries to leave flowers for soldiers. It was called Decoration Day back then. Today, the same act — flowers and flags on a grave — still happens at Arlington and at small-town cemeteries all over the country. Some kinds of remembering don't change much.
