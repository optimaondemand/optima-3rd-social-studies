🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Comparing regions is tricky. It's easy to slip into ranking — "this region is better than that one." Courtesy means describing each region on its own terms. Hockey isn't better than soccer; salsa music isn't better than country music; they're each true to their place. A good comparison shows the differences AND the shared connections.

Learn the Story
📖
Four Regional Panels
What makes each part of the region distinct

This region — North America and the Caribbean — is made up of four big areas. Each one has its own mix of languages, traditions, foods, music, and symbols. Let's walk past each panel.

🍁 Canada is huge — the second-largest country in the world by land. It has two official languages: English and French. The country is governed by a parliament, and the leader is called a Prime Minister. Hockey is the beloved national winter sport. The red maple leaf on the flag stands for the maple trees that grow across the country — and for the maple syrup that comes from them.

🇺🇸 The United States is a mix of many cultures from many parts of the world. English is the most-spoken language, but you'll hear Spanish, Chinese, Vietnamese, Arabic, and many more in everyday life. The flag — the Stars and Stripes — has 50 stars for the 50 states. Baseball is sometimes called the national pastime. American jazz, blues, and country music all started here and traveled around the world.

🌶️ Mexico blends Indigenous heritage (Maya, Aztec, and many others) with Spanish heritage from the colonial period. Spanish is the most-spoken language, but millions of people also speak Indigenous languages like Nahuatl and Maya. The Day of the Dead (Día de Muertos) honors family members who have passed on. Mariachi music, with trumpets and violins, is the national sound. Foods like tortillas, tamales, and chocolate all come from Mexico.

🏝️ The Caribbean is made up of many islands, each with its own culture. Different islands speak different languages — Spanish in Cuba and the Dominican Republic, English in Jamaica and the Bahamas, French in Haiti, Dutch on islands like Curaçao, and Creole languages blending all of them. Reggae, calypso, and salsa music all started in the Caribbean and shaped music around the world. The islands share warm climate, palm-fringed beaches, and a heritage that blends African, Indigenous Taíno, and European cultures.

Even though each region is distinct, they connect in many ways: they share the same continent, many of the same Indigenous peoples whose homelands cross modern borders, and centuries of trade. A song from one region often shows up in another. A food from one region travels through the others.

🤔

Pause & Think: A jazz song born in New Orleans might be played in Toronto, Mexico City, and Havana — all in the same week. What does that tell you about how regions are connected, even though they're different?

Eight cultural characteristics. Four regional panels. Read each card and tap the region where it best fits.

Two official languages — English and French — spoken side by side in this country's government, schools, and signs.
The Day of the Dead (Día de Muertos) — a colorful holiday in early November when families honor relatives who have passed on.
Reggae and calypso music — musical styles born on the islands, with roots in African and local traditions, that spread around the world.
The Stars and Stripes flag with 50 stars — one star for each of the 50 states that make up the country.
Hockey as the national winter sport — played outdoors on frozen ponds and indoors in arenas across this snowy country.
Mariachi music with trumpets, violins, and guitars — a beloved national sound, often played at weddings, birthdays, and big celebrations.
Many islands with many languages — Spanish, English, French, Dutch, and several Creole languages are all spoken across this group of islands.
Baseball as the national pastime — a summer sport with leagues, hot dogs at the park, and a World Series every fall.
🎉 Gallery complete!
🔎 Discovery Fact: Baseball is loved in the United States, but it's also huge in Mexico, Cuba, and the Dominican Republic. Many of the best baseball players in the U.S. major leagues come from the Caribbean. Cultural characteristics belong to a region — but they also travel between regions.
⏳

Then & Now: Hundreds of years ago, these four regional areas were already culturally distinct — different languages, different music, different ways of life. Today, with the internet and travel, cultures share more than ever. Mariachi songs play in Toronto; hockey is played in Florida; reggae fills cafés in Mexico City. The regions are still distinct, but the connections are stronger than ever.

North America and the Caribbean is made of four regional areas — Canada, the United States, Mexico, and the Caribbean — each with its own languages, traditions, music, sports, and symbols. But the region shares a continent, shared histories, and cultures that move freely across borders. A good curator describes each one fairly AND shows what connects them.
