🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
It's easy to call a desert "empty" or the Arctic "boring" — but people live in every one of these climate zones. Courtesy means describing each place fairly. The desert is not less than the rainforest. Each one supports a different kind of life.

Five Climate Zones

1
Learn the Story
📖
A Region with Many Climates
Five climate zones, from far north to far south

Up in Unit 5, you learned that climate is the usual weather of a place over many, many years. Not "what's the weather today?" but "what is the weather usually like here?"

North America and the Caribbean is one region — but it stretches a very long way from north to south. That's why it holds five different climate zones, and each one grows different plants.

❄️ Arctic Tundra sits in far northern Canada. It's freezing cold most of the year. The ground stays frozen so deep that trees can't grow there — only small grasses, moss, and tough little shrubs.

🌲 Boreal Forest covers most of Canada and the northern United States. The winters are long and snowy. Tall evergreen trees like pine and spruce grow here because they can survive cold winters by keeping their needles all year.

🍂 Temperate Plains and Forests cover most of the United States. There are four seasons. The eastern part has deciduous trees (oaks and maples that drop their leaves in the fall). The middle has the Great Plains, where grasses and wheat fields stretch for miles.

🌵 Desert stretches across the southwestern United States and into northern Mexico. It's very hot and very dry. Cacti like the giant saguaro can grow here because they store water inside their thick stems.

🌴 Tropical covers southern Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean islands. It's warm year-round, with heavy rains. Rainforests, palm trees, and bright tropical flowers grow here.

🤔

Pause & Think: All five climate zones are in the same region. But a palm tree could never survive in the Arctic, and a polar bear could never live in the Caribbean. Why do you think climate matters so much for the plants and animals of a place?

Match the Plants to Their Zones
🌿
Map Quest: Match Plants to Climates
Tap each plant, then tap the climate zone where it grows.

Six plants are mixed up across the panels. Read each one and tap the climate zone where it would grow best. The card locks green when you get it right.

Saguaro Cactus — a tall green cactus that can store water inside its thick, ribbed stem.
Coconut Palm — a tall tree with long fronds and coconuts. Loves warm weather and salty sea air.
Spruce Tree — a tall evergreen with sharp needles. Stays green all winter even under heavy snow.
Arctic Moss — a tiny low-growing plant that survives on rocks and frozen ground where trees can't grow.
Maple Tree — a leafy tree that turns bright red and orange in the fall, then drops its leaves before winter.
Banana Plant — a tall plant with huge green leaves that grows fruit. Needs warm weather all year and lots of rain.
🎉 All plants placed!
🔎 Discovery Fact: The giant saguaro cactus can live for more than 150 years and grow over 40 feet tall — but only in the Sonoran Desert in Arizona and northern Mexico. It can't survive a hard freeze, so it stays where winters are mild.
⏳

Then & Now: Long before there were countries or borders, people who lived in each climate zone learned to use the plants and animals around them — pine bark for boats in the boreal forest, agave for food in the desert, palm fronds for roofs in the Caribbean. Today, much of that knowledge is still used. What stayed the same? Climate still shapes how people live in each part of the region.

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. What does the word climate mean?
AThe weather outside right now.
BThe usual weather of a place over many years.
CThe temperature in just one season.
Right! Climate is the long-term pattern of weather in a place — not just today.
Weather is what's happening today. Climate is bigger than that. It's the pattern over many, many years.
2. Where would you find tall evergreen trees like spruce and pine growing naturally?
AThe desert in northern Mexico.
BThe tropical Caribbean islands.
CThe boreal forest across Canada.
Yes! Evergreens like spruce and pine fill the boreal forest — they're tough enough to keep their needles through long, snowy winters.
Evergreens need a cool climate and survive long winters. Which zone has cool weather and snowy winters?
3. Why does the same region have so many different climates?
AThe region stretches a very long way from north to south.
BEvery country chooses its own climate.
CAll four climates are exactly the same temperature.
Exactly! When a region reaches from the Arctic in the north all the way to the warm Caribbean in the south, it crosses many climate zones along the way.
Look at the map again. Canada touches the Arctic. The Caribbean is far to the south. What does that tell you about the size of the region?
