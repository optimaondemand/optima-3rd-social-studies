🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Service
Service means doing real work for people you may never meet. When you design an exhibit, you're helping every visitor who walks in next week, next month, or years from now. Good curators don't put their names in giant letters — they put the evidence first, so the visitor can learn.

Walk Back Through the Museum
🏛️
The Story So Far
Five big ideas across ten rooms

You've worked in ten different rooms this year. Each one taught you something a curator needs to know. Let's look at the five big ideas that came out of all that work.

1. Sources and Questions. In the Curator Training Hall, you learned that a good curator asks before they answer. You sorted primary sources from secondary sources. You looked carefully before making a claim.

2. Places and Maps. In the Great Map Room and the World Map Hall, you learned how to read a map. Titles, compass roses, scales, legends, continents, and oceans — these are the tools that let you talk about where something happened.

3. Regions and People. In the North America Gallery and the United States Regions Gallery, you saw how the land, the climate, and the people of a region all fit together. One country can have many regions, and each one has its own story.

4. Trade and Choices. In Marketplace Hall, you studied scarcity, trade, goods and services, and money. You learned that every choice has a cost and a benefit.

5. Government and Citizenship. In Citizenship Hall, you learned why communities need rules, leaders, and shared symbols. You saw what civic virtues like honesty, courage, and service look like in real life.

🤔

Pause & Think: Which of the five big ideas feels most interesting to you right now? That's a clue about what your exhibit could be about.

Quick Review Cards
🔄
Five-Room Review Cards
Tap each card to check what you remember

Flip each card. If you remember the answer, great. If not, read the back. These five ideas are the ones the museum board cares about most.

What does a curator do before making a claim?
Asks questions and looks closely at the source before deciding what it means.
Name two things every map needs.
A title, a compass rose, a scale, and a legend — any two of these make a map readable.
What makes a region different from a country?
A region is a smaller area inside a country, shaped by the land, climate, and people who live there.
What is scarcity?
When there isn't enough of something to go around, so people have to choose how to use it.
Why do communities need government?
To make rules, settle problems, and protect the rights of the people who live there.
What is one civic virtue?
Honesty, courage, service, or courtesy — any one is a good answer. These are the habits that help communities work.

"Service" in the museum isn't about food. It's about the work a curator does for visitors.
