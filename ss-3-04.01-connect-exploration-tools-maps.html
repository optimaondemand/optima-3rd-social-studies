Remember the Story
📖
The Story So Far
Three rooms, three sets of skills

Think back to where your museum journey started. In the Curator Training Hall, you learned to look at sources carefully. You figured out the difference between primary and secondary sources. You practiced asking good questions before writing anything down.

Then a crate arrived marked "Explorer Routes: Unverified." In the Exploration Wing, you discovered why people explored — for trade, for land, for curiosity. You studied the tools they carried, like compasses and astrolabes. And you learned that exploration had consequences — some good, some harmful.

After that, you entered the Great Map Room. The maps were broken — missing titles, compass roses, scales, and legends. You repaired them. You learned about cardinal directions, map types, scale, and even how flat maps and globes show the world differently.

Now here you are in the Cartographer's Review Chamber. The evidence wall is empty. Your job? Put the pieces together and show the museum how exploration, tools, and maps connect.

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word "cartographer" comes from two old words meaning "map" and "writer." A cartographer is literally a map-writer — and that's exactly what you've been learning to do!
⏳

Then & Now: Long ago, explorers drew maps by hand after sailing for months. Today, satellites take pictures of Earth from space and computers draw maps in seconds. What stayed the same about why people make maps?

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to check what you remember before writing.

1. Why did people explore new lands?
AThey wanted to fix broken maps.
BThey wanted to find trade routes, land, and new things.
CThey wanted to build museums.
That's right! Explorers set out to find trade routes, new land, and things they hadn't seen before.
Think back to the Exploration Wing. What reasons did we learn about for why people explored?
2. How did tools like the compass help explorers?
AThey helped explorers figure out which direction they were going.
BThey told explorers how far away land was.
CThey showed explorers pictures of new places.
Exactly! A compass points to north, which helped explorers know which direction they were heading.
Remember what a compass does — it always points in one direction. How would that help someone at sea?
3. Why are map elements like titles, legends, and compass roses important?
AThey make the map look pretty.
BThey tell you who drew the map.
CThey help the reader understand what the map shows.
Yes! Map elements help anyone reading the map understand the title, symbols, directions, and distances.
Think about the Map Room — what happened when maps were missing their elements? Could visitors understand them?
