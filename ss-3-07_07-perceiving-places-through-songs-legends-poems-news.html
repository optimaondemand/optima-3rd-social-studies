🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Stories belong to the people who tell them. A folk song from the West was sung by real people around real campfires. A Cherokee legend has been told by Cherokee families for generations. Courtesy at the Listening Station means we slow down, listen first, and treat each story as a gift from the people who made it.

How Stories Tell Us About Places
🎬
Teacher recording notes: Explain that there are four kinds of stories at the station today: (1) Songs — folk songs people sang while working, traveling, or remembering home. (2) Legends — old stories told and retold, sometimes about real people, sometimes about made-up heroes. (3) Poems — short writing that captures how a place feels. Four Kinds of Stories from the Regions

The Listening Station holds four stories today — one song, one legend, one poem, and one news clipping. Each one comes from a different region. As you read each card, ask yourself: What does this story tell me about the place it comes from?

🎵 STORY 1 · A FOLK SONG
A famous old American folk song called "Home on the Range" describes a place where buffalo roam, deer and antelope play, and the sky is wide and clear. People sang it on long cattle drives and around campfires. It became Kansas's official state song in 1947.

What's this song about? Wide open land, animals, big skies, and ranching life.

🎧
Press play to hear "Home on the Range"
🪵 STORY 2 · A TALL TALE LEGEND
The legend of Paul Bunyan tells of a giant lumberjack so big he could chop down a hundred trees with one swing of his axe. Stories say his footprints made the Great Lakes. People in lumber camps in the upper Midwest and Northwest told these tall tales while they worked in the huge pine forests.

What's this legend about? Huge forests, hard work, and the kind of imagination that grew up in lumber country.

🪵 How Paul Bunyan Made the Mississippi

One winter, the snow in Minnesota fell so deep that Paul Bunyan's beard froze solid. His ox, Babe (who was blue from the cold), couldn't even find the path home. Paul scratched his chilly head and said, "Babe, we need water — and we need it to keep moving, all the way to the sea."

So Paul grabbed his giant water pail, walked from Minnesota all the way down to Louisiana, and dragged that pail behind him the whole way. The pail had a tiny hole in the bottom, and a thin trickle leaked out as he walked. Babe stomped along beside him, plodding south.

By the time Paul reached the Gulf of Mexico, the trickle had grown into a river — a mighty river that kept flowing long after Paul went home. People called it the Mississippi. And every spring, when the snow melts up north and the river runs high, old lumberjacks wink and say, "That must be Paul, walking home again."

Traditional tall tale, retold for the Listening Station. Stories like this one were swapped around campfires and bunkhouses in the lumber camps of the upper Midwest and Northwest.

📜 STORY 3 · A POEM
A poet named Carl Sandburg wrote a famous poem in 1914 simply called "Chicago." In it, he describes the city as a busy worker town, full of railroads, stockyards, and tough, hardworking people. The poem makes Chicago sound proud, noisy, and alive.

What's this poem about? A big Midwest city, busy with trains, workers, and industry.

📜 From "Chicago" by Carl Sandburg (1914)

The opening lines that made the poem famous:

Hog Butcher for the World,
Tool Maker, Stacker of Wheat,
Player with Railroads and the Nation's Freight Handler;
Stormy, husky, brawling,
City of the Big Shoulders… — Carl Sandburg, "Chicago" (1914) · Public domain

What's Sandburg saying? Chicago was where pigs were butchered for the country, where tools were made, where wheat was stacked high, and where railroads moved goods everywhere. He called it the "City of the Big Shoulders" because it carried so much of America's hardest work on its back.

"Husky" and "brawling" are old-fashioned ways of saying strong and a little rough. Sandburg meant it as a compliment.

📰 STORY 4 · A NEWS HEADLINE
A 1965 newspaper headline read: "Hurricane Betsy Pounds Louisiana Coast — Floodwaters Rise in New Orleans." The article told how thousands of families left their homes and how the city pulled together to help neighbors. News stories like this one show how Gulf Coast communities live with powerful storms.

What's this news about? Hurricane country — and how Southeast communities respond when big storms hit.

The Gulf Coast Times
★ Serving Louisiana and the Coast Since 1879 ★
Vol. 86 · No. 254 New Orleans, La. · Friday, September 10, 1965 Price: 10¢
HURRICANE BETSY POUNDS LOUISIANA COAST
Floodwaters Rise in New Orleans · Thousands Take Shelter as Neighbors Help Neighbors
By Special Correspondent

Hurricane Betsy roared ashore late Wednesday night with winds clocked at over 125 miles per hour, leaving large parts of southeast Louisiana underwater and many homes in the Lower Ninth Ward damaged or destroyed. Families who had been told to evacuate took shelter at schools and churches across the city.

By Thursday morning, fishermen along the bayou had launched their boats — not to fish, but to help search for neighbors stranded on rooftops. The Coast Guard joined the effort by the afternoon. "When the water came up, we just started knocking on doors," said one volunteer who paddled into a flooded neighborhood with his cousin.

Local officials reported that thousands of residents were without power and would likely remain so for days. Donations of canned food, dry clothing, and clean water were already pouring in from across the South. Schools throughout the region planned to remain closed through the weekend.

For neighbors helping neighbors and information on shelters, see Page 3.

Note: This is a pretend newspaper page, written for the Listening Station. Hurricane Betsy was a real storm that hit Louisiana on September 9, 1965 — and the community response described here reflects what really happened.

🔎 Discovery Fact
The folk song "Home on the Range" was probably written in the 1870s by a Kansas doctor named Brewster Higley. It traveled west by word of mouth — cowboys would sing it on long cattle drives, changing words as they went.

The Big Picture
🏷️ Exhibit Label

Places are understood through more than maps. Songs, legends, poems, and news show us how people feel about where they live — what they love, what they fear, what they remember, what they celebrate. Together, maps and stories make a region come alive in the visitor's mind.

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions before writing your Map Quest response.

1. What can a song or a poem tell us about a place that a map cannot?
AThe exact distance between two cities
BThe names of every state
CHow people feel about the place — what they love, miss, or remember
Right! Maps show where. Stories show how people feel about a place.
Distances and state names are what maps are good at. Think about what makes a song or poem powerful — it's about feelings.
2. The Paul Bunyan tall tale about a giant lumberjack most likely comes from which kind of region?
AA hot desert region
BA region with huge forests and lumber camps
CA region near the ocean only
Yes! Paul Bunyan stories grew up among people who worked in the great forests of the upper Midwest and Northwest.
A giant lumberjack story belongs in a place with lots of big trees. Think about which kind of region has those forests.
3. Why is courtesy important when we listen to stories from a region?
ABecause the stories belong to real people who created and shared them
BBecause we should pick favorites and ignore the rest
CBecause old stories are not true
Exactly! Every story comes from real people. Courtesy means we listen first, treat the stories with care, and never say one region's stories are "better" than another's.
A good curator never ignores stories or says one region is better. Think about whose stories these are.
