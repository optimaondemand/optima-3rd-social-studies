🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Courtesy in a museum means making your exhibit easy for visitors to read. Big enough labels. Clear words. No showing off with fancy language. Curators write for the visitor, not for themselves. That's what courtesy looks like at the museum.

The Four Parts
🧩
A Strong Exhibit Has Four Parts
Each one answers a question a visitor will ask

Every panel in this museum follows the same four-part shape. That's so visitors can compare exhibits and learn quickly. Here's what each part does:

1. Map evidence. This shows the visitor where. Pick a place you've studied — a country, a region, a city, or even a continent. Add a title, a compass rose, and a small label that points to the spot. A visitor should be able to find it in seconds.

2. Source evidence. This shows the visitor who said so. Use a photo, a chart, a quote, or a primary document. Real curators don't just say things — they back it up. Your source proves your exhibit isn't just a story; it's about a real place with real people.

3. Community connection. This shows the visitor why it matters to people. What do the people of this place do for work? What do they eat? What do they celebrate? How is their government or their region different from yours? This is the heart of the exhibit.

4. Civic virtue. This shows the visitor what we can learn. Pick a virtue — honesty, courage, service, courtesy — and explain how the people or the place show that virtue. This is your invitation to the visitor: "Look here. See what we can be."

Strong Labels vs. Weak Labels
🔄
Label Practice Cards
Tap to flip — see a weak label, then the strong version

A visitor only has a few seconds at each exhibit. Your labels have to do a lot of work in a little space. Flip each card to see how a weak label can be made strong.

Weak:
"This is a map."
Strong:
"This map shows the Northeast Region of the U.S. — where many big cities and old harbors are."
Weak:
"A picture of money."
Strong:
"A photo of U.S. dollars and Mexican pesos — proof that different countries make different money."
Weak:
"People live here."
Strong:
"Farmers in the Midwest grow food that travels all over the country — so their work feeds far away communities."
Weak:
"They show courage."
Strong:
"This community shows courage by helping each other rebuild after hurricanes."
Weak:
"This is important."
Strong:
"Voting matters because it lets a whole community choose who makes the rules — not just one person."
Weak:
"A chart of stuff."
Strong:
"A chart showing how much rain the Caribbean gets each month — it explains why some crops grow well there."

Then & Now: In old museums, exhibit labels were tiny, written in cursive, and used hard words. Today's labels are simple, short, and friendly. What changed? Curators learned that an exhibit is only as good as the words a visitor can read.

1. How many parts does a strong exhibit have?

BTwo — a map and a label.
CFour — map, source, community connection, and virtue.
Right! Four parts: a map (where), a source (who said so), a community connection (why it matters), and a virtue (what we can learn).

2. Which is a strong exhibit label?
A"This is a thing about a place."
B"This map shows the Southeast Region — where warm weather lets farmers grow oranges and peaches."
C"Map."
Yes! A strong label tells the visitor what they're seeing, where it is, and why it matters — all in one sentence.
Look again. Which label tells a visitor what the map is showing and why it matters?
3. What does "courtesy" mean for an exhibit designer?
AWriting labels that are clear and easy for visitors to read.
BUsing big, fancy words to sound smart.
CHiding the answers so visitors have to work for them.
Exactly! Courtesy means writing for the visitor — clear, kind, easy to read. No showing off.
Think about why the director cares about clear labels. Whose job is the exhibit really for?

Curator Task: Build the Exhibit
Write the four labels for your panel.

Write four short labels — one for each part of your Community-in-the-World Exhibit. Each label is one or two clear sentences a visitor could read. Use the sentence starters to help you.

1. Map Label — Tell the visitor what the map shows.

"This map shows ____, where ____."

2. Source Label — Tell the visitor what the source proves.

"This ____ (photo / chart / quote / document) shows ____."

3. Community Connection Label — Tell the visitor what people do, eat, celebrate, or build here.

"In this community, people ____ because ____."

4. Virtue Label — Tell the visitor what virtue this place shows.

"This exhibit shows the virtue of ____ because ____."
