🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
A trade map only works if the mapmaker is honest about each region. If Florida claims it grows wheat when it doesn't, or if the Caribbean hides that it has sugar, traders make bad deals. Today, notice how an honest map shows what each place really has — and what it really needs.

Learn the Story
📖
Why Goods Travel
Region, resource, route — the same scarcity story on a bigger map

Remember the coastal town and the valley farm? One had plenty of fish but no grain. The other had plenty of grain but no fish. So they traded. The same thing happens between whole regions — just on a much bigger map.

Each region of North America and the Caribbean has its own resources — the things the land or water gives that people use to make goods. Canada has huge forests and oil, so it sends timber and fuel south. The U.S. prairies grow wheat and corn for the whole country and beyond. Florida grows oranges that ship out to grocery stores far away from any orange tree. Mexico grows avocados and peppers and has silver in its mountains. The Caribbean islands grow sugarcane, bananas, and coffee in warm weather no Northern region has.

A trade route is a path goods travel from where they come from to where they're wanted. A trade route map shows those paths as arrows. Long ago the arrows were ships and wagons. Today they are trucks, trains, planes, and cargo ships — but the reason the arrows exist hasn't changed: each region has scarcity of something, and somewhere else has plenty.

Source Study
A Trade Route Map of the Americas
🖼️ A clear, kid-friendly trade route or resource map showing North America (the United States, Canada, Mexico) and the Caribbean, with arrows or labels showing what each region produces and where goods travel. A historical trade routes map from the 1800s–1900s (showing sugar, cotton, timber, or fur routes) works beautifully and ties to earlier exploration lessons. A modern colorful "products of the Americas" classroom map is also a great fit — they often show oranges in Florida, wheat in the prairies, oil in Canada, silver in Mexico, and sugarcane in the Caribbean.

Where to find one: Library of Congress Digital Collections (loc.gov) — search "trade routes map Americas," "products map United States," or "commodity map North America"; Wikimedia Commons (commons.wikimedia.org) — search "North America resources map" or "Caribbean trade map"; the David Rumsey Map Collection (davidrumsey.com) — search "products of the Americas" or "trade routes."

Quick Self-Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. What is a trade route?
AA line that shows where two countries share a border.
BThe path goods travel from a region with plenty to a region with scarcity.
CA road that only one country can use.
Yes! A trade route is the path a resource takes from where it's plentiful to where it's needed.
Think about the arrows on the map. They don't show borders — they show where goods go. Which option matches that?
2. Why don't Canadian shoppers grow their own oranges instead of buying them from Florida?
ABecause Canadians don't like oranges.
BBecause Canada has a law against oranges.
CBecause oranges don't grow well in Canada's cold climate — that's a scarcity.
Exactly! Oranges need warmth. Canada's climate is too cold, so it has scarcity of citrus — and Florida has plenty.
Think back to the resources table. What kind of weather do oranges need? What kind of weather does Canada have?
3. Why does honesty matter on a trade route map?
AIf the map shows a resource in the wrong place, traders make bad choices and lose time and money.
BBecause honest maps are always more colorful.
CBecause honesty makes the paper stronger.
Right! A trade map is evidence. If it's not honest, traders end up where the resource isn't, and trade breaks down.
Think about today's virtue. What goes wrong for a trader who follows a dishonest map?
