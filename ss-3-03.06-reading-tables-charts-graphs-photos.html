🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Self-Governance
Self-governance means making good choices on your own. When curators decide which chart, graph, or photo to study, they're governing their own thinking. They don't grab the first thing they see — they stop, think about the question, and pick the evidence that actually helps.

Learn the Story
📖
Evidence Is More Than Maps
Tables, charts, graphs, and photos each tell a different part of the story

A map can show you where the Amazon River is — but it can't tell you how long it is or how much water flows through it. For that, you need a table or a chart. Each type of evidence answers a different kind of question.

Think of the Great Map Room like a team. The maps show where. The tables show what facts. The charts show how much. The graphs show how things change. And the photos show what it really looks like. A good curator reads all of them — not just the maps.

Remember back in Unit 1 when you learned to study sources carefully — looking at what kind of evidence it is before deciding what it proves? The same skill works here. Before you read a chart or a graph, ask yourself: "What kind of information does this give me?"

🤔

Pause & Think: If a visitor asked, "Has the world's population been going up or down?" — would you show them a table, a bar chart, a line graph, or a photo? Why?

Investigate the Evidence
📊
Evidence Type Gallery
Tap each card to learn what this kind of evidence does

Each piece of evidence on the wall answers a different kind of question. Tap a card to see what it does best.

Table
tap to flip
A table organizes facts into rows and columns. It's great for comparing details side by side — like the names, lengths, and locations of the world's longest rivers.
Bar Chart
tap to flip
A bar chart uses bars of different heights to compare amounts. Taller bar = more. It answers questions like "Which continent has the most people?"
Line Graph
tap to flip
A line graph shows how something changes over time. The line goes up when a number increases and down when it decreases. Great for tracking temperature, population, or rainfall over months or years.
Photograph
tap to flip
A photograph shows what a place or thing really looks like. It captures details that drawings and charts can't — the color of the water, the shape of the mountains, the buildings in a city.
