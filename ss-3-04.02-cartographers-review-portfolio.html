1
What Goes in a Portfolio?
📖
Your Portfolio Has Three Sections
One for each part of your museum journey

A portfolio is like a folder of your best work. The museum board wants to see that you learned real skills — not just facts you memorized, but things you can do.

Section 1: Evidence Skills — In the Curator Training Hall, you learned to study sources carefully. You sorted primary sources from secondary sources. You asked good questions before making claims. These are your evidence skills.

Section 2: Exploration Knowledge — In the Exploration Wing, you learned why people explored, what tools they used, and what happened because of their journeys. You found out that exploration had consequences — some helpful, some harmful.

Section 3: Map Skills — In the Great Map Room, you repaired maps by adding titles, compass roses, scales, and legends. You learned directions, map types, and how scale shows distance. These are your map skills.

🤔

Pause & Think: Which section feels strongest for you — evidence skills, exploration knowledge, or map skills? Which one do you want to practice more?

Review Your Evidence
🔄
Portfolio Review Cards
Tap each card to check what you remember

Flip each card to review a key idea. If you remember it, great! If not, read the back and think about it before you write your portfolio.

What is a primary source?
Something made during the time it describes — like a photo, a letter, or a map from that time period.
Why did people explore?
They wanted to find trade routes, claim new land, spread ideas, or discover things nobody had seen before.
What does a compass rose show?
It shows directions on a map — north, south, east, west, and the directions in between.
What is one consequence of exploration?
Explorers found new places and trade routes, but they also took land from people who already lived there.
Why do maps need a legend?
A legend explains what the symbols on a map mean, so anyone can read and understand it.
How does map scale help?
Scale tells you how distances on the map compare to real distances, so you know how far apart places really are.
⏳

Then & Now: Long ago, a portfolio was a large leather case that artists and mapmakers carried to show their work. Today, students and workers still use portfolios — sometimes on paper, sometimes on a computer. What stayed the same about why people collect their best work?

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions before writing your portfolio synthesis.

1. What makes something a primary source?
AIt was written in a textbook about the past.
BIt is the most important source on a topic.
CIt was made during the time it describes.
Right! A primary source comes from the actual time period — like a map, photo, or letter from that era.
Think about what "primary" means here. Is it about importance, or about when it was made?
2. How are exploration and maps connected?
AExplorers invented maps before they traveled.
BExplorers found new places, and mapmakers recorded them.
CMaps told explorers exactly where to go.
Yes! Explorers discovered new lands and routes, and mapmakers drew what was found so everyone could learn from it.
Think about the order: Did explorers already have perfect maps, or did maps get better because of what explorers found?
3. What is a portfolio?
AA collection of your best evidence, organized to show what you know.
BA test with right and wrong answers.
CA folder of blank paper for drawing.
Exactly! A portfolio organizes your best evidence so others can see what you've learned and done.
A portfolio isn't a test. It's more like a folder where you show your best thinking and evidence.
