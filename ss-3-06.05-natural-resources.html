🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
It's easy to write a label that only brags about a resource — "Texas has lots of oil!" — and skip what's tricky. A good curator tells the whole story: what the resource is, how people use it, and one honest thing about its limits. Honest labels help visitors understand the real choices people make.

Learn the Story
📖
What Is a Natural Resource?
And why do curators split them into two groups?

A natural resource is something useful that comes from nature — not from a factory. Trees, water, fish, soil, oil, copper, sunlight, wind, and gold are all natural resources.

North America and the Caribbean is rich in natural resources. The huge boreal forests of Canada give wood and paper. The Great Plains of the United States give wheat and corn. The Great Lakes hold one-fifth of all the freshwater on Earth. Caribbean islands grow sugarcane and bananas in their warm soil. Mexico has silver and copper mines. Texas and the Gulf of Mexico have oil.

Curators split natural resources into two big groups:

🌱 Renewable resources can grow back or refill — if people are careful. Trees can grow back if new ones are planted. Wheat fields grow again every year. Freshwater is refilled by rain. Fish can have more babies. But each of these needs care. A forest cut down too fast doesn't grow back in one year — it takes 30 or more. A river polluted today won't be clean again tomorrow.

⛏️ Nonrenewable resources are different. Once they're used up, they're gone for a very long time — millions of years. Oil, coal, copper, silver, and iron all formed deep in the Earth over millions of years. People can dig them up, but no one can make more.

This is why honesty matters. A label that says "Texas has plenty of oil!" leaves out the most important part: oil is nonrenewable. Every barrel pumped is one less in the ground forever.

🤔

Pause & Think: A forest is renewable, but only if people give it time to grow back. What does that tell you about how people should use renewable resources, even though they can grow again?

Sort the Shelf
🧺
Map Quest: Renewable or Nonrenewable?
Tap each resource, then tap the group it belongs to.

Six resources from across the region are on the shelf. For each, tap whether it is Renewable (can grow back or refill) or Nonrenewable (gone for a very long time once used up).

Oil (petroleum) — pumped from underground in Texas, Mexico, and Canada. Used for gasoline, heating, and plastic. Formed over millions of years.
Forests (timber) — huge pine and spruce forests across Canada and the northern U.S. Used for lumber, paper, and furniture. Trees grow back if replanted.
Copper ore — mined from rock in Mexico and the U.S. Used for electrical wires and pipes. Formed deep in the Earth long ago.
Wheat (and other crops) — grown on the Great Plains farms of the U.S. and Canada. Planted, harvested, and replanted every year.
Freshwater — held in the Great Lakes, the Mississippi River, and Canadian lakes. Refilled by rain and snow each year.
Fish — caught off the Atlantic and Pacific coasts and around the Caribbean islands. Fish have more babies if there are enough adults left.
🎉 Shelf sorted!
🔎 Discovery Fact: Most renewable resources can still run out if people use them too fast. If too many fish are caught one year, there aren't enough adults left to make babies. That's why honest curators say "renewable" comes with a rule: take care of it, or you'll lose it.
⏳

Then & Now: Hundreds of years ago, Indigenous peoples across this region used resources carefully because they had to — they couldn't replace what they wasted. Today, machines let us use much more, much faster. What stayed the same? Resources still run out if they're not cared for. Curators tell this story honestly.

A natural resource is something useful that comes from nature. North America and the Caribbean is rich in resources — forests, crops, freshwater, fish, minerals, and oil. Renewable resources can grow back or refill with care. Nonrenewable resources are gone for a very long time once used. Honest curators describe both.

🦉
🧭 Opti says…
Tricky thought for curators: gold is rare, but it's nonrenewable — once dug up, no more is made. Sunshine is everywhere and free, and it's renewable — it shines again tomorrow. "Rare" and "renewable" aren't the same thing! That's why we always check honestly.
