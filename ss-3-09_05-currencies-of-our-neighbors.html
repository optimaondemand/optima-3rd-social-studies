🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
A respectful curator never laughs at someone else's money. The pictures, the words, and even the colors on a Canadian dollar or a Mexican peso were chosen carefully by people who care about their country. Today, notice how a courteous curator describes each currency the way its own people would describe it.

Four Currencies of North America and the Caribbean
Same four rules — different countries

Walk into a store in the United States and you'll pay with U.S. dollars. Walk into a store in Canada, just across the northern border, and the dollars look different. Cross into Mexico to the south and the money is called something else again. Travel to the islands of the Caribbean and each country has its own currency too.

The United States uses the U.S. dollar. Bills are green and have presidents on them. Coins come in cents — pennies, nickels, dimes, and quarters.

In Canada, people use the Canadian dollar. The bills are colorful — red, purple, blue, green — and one-dollar coins are nicknamed "loonies" because they show a water bird called a loon. Two-dollar coins are called "toonies."

In Mexico, the currency is the Mexican peso. The word peso means "weight" in Spanish — a reminder of older times when coins were valued by how much they weighed.

The Caribbean has many countries, and many of them use their own money. Jamaica uses the Jamaican dollar. Cuba uses the Cuban peso. Eight smaller island nations share one currency called the Eastern Caribbean dollar. Each currency lasts, is easy to carry, is easy to count, and is trusted by the people in its country.

Quick Self-Check

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. What is the name of the currency used in Mexico?
AThe Mexican dollar.
BThe Mexican peso.
CThe Mexican loonie.
Right! The Mexican peso. The word peso means "weight" in Spanish.
Almost — the word for Mexico's money comes from Spanish, and it means "weight." Try again.
2. Even though Canada and the United States both use "dollars," why are they not the same currency?
ABecause the bills are the same size in both countries.
BBecause they share one government.
CBecause each country prints and trusts its own money — they look different and can be worth different amounts.
Yes! Same word, different country, different money. Each country's dollar is trusted only in its own country.
Think about the four rules of money — especially the last one. Whose trust makes the Canadian dollar work?
3. What is courteous about the way a curator describes another country's money?
AThe curator uses the country's own name for the currency and respects the pictures on it.
BThe curator tries to convince visitors that one currency is better than the others.
CThe curator changes the color of the bills before putting them in the case.
Exactly! A courteous curator uses the correct names and treats every country's money with the same respect.
Think about today's virtue — courtesy. Which choice shows respect for another country's people and their money?
