Word Roots
Latin Root: constitu- — "to set up, to establish"

The Latin root constitu- means "to set up" or "to put in place." When you constitute something, you build it on purpose. That's exactly what the writers of the U.S. Constitution were doing — setting up a whole country from scratch.

📖

🔎 Discovery Fact
The U.S. Constitution is one of the shortest constitutions in the world — only about 4,500 words. But it has set up the rules for the United States since 1787.

Learn the Story
📖
A Country Built From a Piece of Paper
Why 1787 still matters

In the summer of 1787, leaders from across the brand-new United States traveled to a city called Philadelphia. The country had only been free from Britain for a few years, and it was a mess. Each state had its own rules and its own money. Some states wouldn't even trade with each other. The leaders met inside one hot building all summer and wrote down a plan: the Constitution.

A constitution is the written rules that set up a government. It tells you who can make laws, who can enforce them, who can decide if they're fair, and what rights belong to the people no matter what. The U.S. Constitution did something brand new for the time: it said the country's power would come from the people, not from a king.

That's what those first three words mean — "We the People." The Constitution doesn't begin with the name of a ruler. It begins with everybody. The people make the country, the people choose the leaders, and the leaders work for the people. We call this idea power from the people, or sometimes self-government.

Here's the part curators love: even the President of the United States has to follow the Constitution. The most powerful person in the country still has to obey the same set of rules. That's how a Constitution protects everyone — by being above everyone.

🤔

Pause & Think: Think about the words "the most powerful person still has to follow the rules." Why is that idea so important for keeping a country fair?

Council Chamber
A new country is being built. Should one person decide all the rules, or should the people decide together?
👑 Side A: One Strong Leader

A king or queen could make decisions quickly, with no arguing. Everyone would know who's in charge. If the leader is wise, the country runs smoothly. There would be no long debates or slow votes when the country needs to act fast.

🤝 Side B: Power from the People

The people vote and choose their own leaders. No single person has all the power, so no single person can become a tyrant. Even the top leader has to follow the same rules everyone else does. Decisions take longer, but they belong to everybody — not just one family.

The leaders in 1787 chose Side B. They wrote the U.S. Constitution to set up a government with power from the people. Look at both sides carefully. Notice what each one gains, and what each one loses. In Tab 4, you'll write your own Council Chamber recommendation — using evidence from this lesson to defend your choice.

⏳

Then & Now: In 1787, only some people could vote — and that wasn't fair. Over the years, the Constitution was changed (we call those changes amendments) to let more people vote. Today, almost every adult citizen can vote. The Constitution is still doing its job, but the people kept making it better.
