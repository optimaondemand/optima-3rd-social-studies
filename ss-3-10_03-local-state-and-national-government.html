Word Roots
Latin Root: loc- — "place"

The Latin root loc- means "place." Hear it? It's hiding inside the word local, which means "having to do with the place you live." A local government is the government for your place — your town or city.

📖

local — having to do with your place or area · location — a specific place · locate — to find the place of something · locality — the area around a specific place

🎉 All matched! Now you know why local government is about your place.
🔎 Discovery Fact
The word locomotive — a train engine — also uses this root. It literally means "something that moves itself from place to place."

Three Levels, Three Different Jobs
🎬
Teacher recording notes: Walk students through the three levels from the bottom up: local (city/county — closest to home, runs schools, libraries, fire stations), state (Tallahassee for Florida — runs driver's licenses, state parks, state roads), national (Washington, D.C. — runs the military, money, the post office, deals with other countries). Learn the Story
📖
Climbing the Model
Local → State → National

Stand back from the model for a second. You can see right away that the United States doesn't have just one government — it has three levels, and each one is in charge of different things. They're stacked up, but they don't boss each other around. They cooperate — they each do their part.

🏙️ Bottom level — Local government. This is the government for your place — your city, town, or county. Local government meets at a city hall or county courthouse. The leader is usually called a mayor. Local government runs the things you see every day: your public school, the trash truck, the fire station, the public library, and the parks where you play.

🌴 Middle level — State government. This is the government for a whole state. In Florida, that means the government in Tallahassee, the state capital. The leader is called a governor. State government runs things that are bigger than one town but smaller than the whole country — driver's licenses, state highways, state parks (like the Everglades), and the rules for things like fishing and hunting.

🇺🇸 Top level — National government. Also called the federal government. This one is for the whole United States. It meets in Washington, D.C., in the white-domed U.S. Capitol you saw last week. The leader is the President. The national government does jobs that have to be the same for everybody in the country — printing money, running the military, the post office, and making agreements with other countries.

Here's the part that surprises a lot of curators: the three levels work together. Your school is run locally — but it follows some state rules and some national rules at the same time. That's how three levels can serve one community.

🤔

Pause & Think: Picture your typical morning — waking up, eating breakfast, riding to school. Which level of government showed up in your morning, and how?

⏳

Then & Now: When the United States started in 1776, there were only 13 states, and most jobs were done by towns and states. Today there are 50 states and a much bigger country, so the national level handles a lot more. The three-level idea has stayed the same — but the size of each level has grown.
