🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Honesty
Honesty for a museum guide isn't just telling the truth. It's saying what you actually know — no big words to sound smart, no made-up details to fill space. The best curators say "I'm not sure, but here's my best guess" as often as they say "I know." Visitors trust that kind of honesty.

1
The Five Steps of a Walk-Through
🗣️
What a Great Walk-Through Sounds Like
Five steps, in order, that work every time

A walk-through is what you say out loud when a visitor stops at your panel. The same five-step shape works for every exhibit. Here it is:

1. Greet. Just say hi. "Hi — welcome to my exhibit." That's it. You don't need a long opener. Visitors like a real greeting better than a fancy one.

2. Show the map. Point to it. Say what place it shows. Tell why you picked it. "This map shows the Southeast Region of the U.S. I picked it because that's where I live, and it's warmer than most other parts of the country."

3. Explain the source. Tell what kind of source it is and what it proves. "This is a photo of a real orange grove. It proves that warm weather in the Southeast really does help farmers grow citrus."

4. Point to the community connection. Tell the visitor why this matters to real people. "In this community, farmers grow oranges that get shipped to the rest of the country, so people far away can eat fruit from our region."

5. End with the virtue and invite a question. "I picked the virtue of service because farmers feed people they may never meet. Do you have any questions?"

That's the whole shape. Five steps. You can do it in about a minute. The best part: the same shape works whether your exhibit is about a region, a country, a marketplace, or a community.

Honest vs. Polished Curators
🔄
Honesty in a Walk-Through
Tap to compare a "polished" guide and an "honest" guide

Some curators try to sound perfect, like they know everything. Honest curators sound real — they say what they know and admit what they don't. Flip each card to see the difference.

Polished:
"This is, without doubt, the most important map ever made."
Honest:
"I picked this map because it shows where my community is — and it has every map element a visitor needs to read it."
Polished:
"Yes, I know everything about this region."
Honest:
"I know a lot about this region. There are a few things I'm still figuring out — like exactly how the climate has changed over time."
Polished:
"Every person in this community shows great courage."
Honest:
"Many people in this community show courage — like the way neighbors help each other after storms. I'm sure not every single person does, but lots of them do."
Polished:
"I have no questions left. I know it all."
Honest:
"I still have questions. That's part of being a curator — you never run out of things to wonder about."
Visitor asks:
"What's the population here?"
Honest answer:
"Good question — I'm not sure of the exact number, but I know it's one of the bigger communities in the region."
Visitor asks:
"Why this virtue and not another?"
Honest answer:
"I thought about a few virtues, but this one fit best. The people of this place show it in real ways, not just in stories."
⏳

Then & Now: Long ago, museum guides wore stuffy uniforms and gave the same speech to every visitor — no questions allowed. Today's guides talk like real people and let visitors ask things back. What changed? Curators learned that the visitor's curiosity is the most important thing in the room.
