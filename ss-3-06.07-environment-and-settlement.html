🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Some communities chose their spot for trade, some for safety, some for farming. None of those reasons is "better" than the others — they were the right choice for the right place. Courtesy means describing each settlement on its own terms, not ranking them.

Four Big Reasons People Settle
🎬

Learn the Story
📖
Why People Build Where They Do
Four reasons that come up over and over

Cities don't just appear randomly. People settle in places that give them something the land around them needs. Across thousands of years, the same four reasons come up over and over in this region.

🌊 Water for trade. A river or natural harbor lets boats bring goods in and carry goods out. Cities on water grow because food, supplies, and trade come right to them. The mouth of the Mississippi River (New Orleans), the bay of San Francisco, and the protected harbor of Havana all became big cities for this reason.

🌾 Good soil and freshwater. A place with rich farmland and a lake or river to drink from can feed a lot of people. The Aztec people built their capital — what we now call Mexico City — in a high valley with a freshwater lake and fertile soil, because it could feed a huge city.

⛰️ Resource access. Some cities grew because something valuable was nearby. Denver, Colorado, sits at the foot of the Rocky Mountains. When gold was discovered there in the 1850s, miners poured in, and the city boomed. The mountains held the resource; Denver was the doorway.

🛡️ Easy to defend. Long ago, before peace treaties and modern armies, communities needed to be safe from attack. A high cliff or a peninsula made a town much easier to defend. Quebec City was built on a tall cliff above the St. Lawrence River — anyone trying to attack had to climb up.

Many cities have more than one reason. Quebec City has the cliff (defense) and the river (trade). Mexico City has soil and water. Curators look for the strongest reason — but they pay attention to the others too.

🤔

Pause & Think: If you were leading a group of families looking for a place to start a new town a thousand years ago, what would you want the land around you to have? Why?

Match the Pins
🧩
Map Quest: Match the City to Its Reason
Tap each city, then tap the environmental reason it grew there.

Six cities, four big reasons. Read each one carefully and pick the strongest environmental reason it became a major settlement.

New Orleans, Louisiana, USA — sits right where the giant Mississippi River meets the Gulf of Mexico. Boats can come up the river from the ocean and travel deep into North America.
Mexico City, Mexico — built in a high valley with rich volcanic soil and a freshwater lake. The Aztec people built their huge capital here over 700 years ago because the land could feed many people.
Denver, Colorado, USA — sits at the foot of the Rocky Mountains, where the plains meet the peaks. Gold was discovered nearby in 1858, and miners flooded in. The city grew as the doorway to the mountains.
Quebec City, Canada — built on a tall rocky cliff high above the St. Lawrence River. The high ground made it nearly impossible to attack. French settlers chose the spot in 1608 partly because it could be guarded.
San Francisco, California, USA — wraps around one of the largest deep natural harbors in the Pacific Ocean. Big ships from across the world can dock right at the city.
Havana, Cuba — has a long, narrow harbor that opens to the Caribbean Sea. The shape of the harbor protects boats from storms. For over 500 years, Havana has been a busy trade port.
🎉 Map matched!
🔎 Discovery Fact: Three of the six cities here grew up next to water — that's not an accident. Around the world, most of the biggest cities are still on a river or a coast. People have always followed the water.
⏳

Then & Now: The reasons people chose these spots hundreds of years ago — water, soil, resources, safety — still matter today. New Orleans is still a giant shipping port. Mexico City still has farmland feeding it. Denver still serves the Rocky Mountain region. The environment shaped where cities grew, and the cities then shaped how people live there.

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions before writing your evidence responses.

1. Why did so many big cities in this region grow next to a river or harbor?
APeople didn't want to walk too far for a swim.
BBoats let goods move in and out — water means trade.
CRivers always had the best weather.
Right! Water means trade. Boats can bring food and supplies and carry goods out — cities on water can grow much bigger.
Think about what a river or harbor lets people do that flat dry land doesn't. Hint: it has to do with moving things in and out.
2. Why did Denver, Colorado, grow into a big city?
AIt is the only city in the United States with a river.
BThe land near Denver is great for growing bananas.
CGold was discovered in the nearby Rocky Mountains.
Yes! Denver sits at the foot of the Rockies, where gold was found in 1858. Miners flooded in, and the city became the doorway to the mountains.
Many U.S. cities are near rivers, and bananas grow in tropical climates, not Colorado. What did the Rocky Mountains have that drew people in?
3. Why was Quebec City built on top of a tall cliff?
AIt was easier to defend from attack.
BThe cliff blocked the wind.
CIt was the only place that wasn't snowy.
Exactly! French settlers chose the spot in 1608 partly because the cliff made the town much harder to attack.
Think about what a high cliff does for safety. Long ago, before modern defenses, what advantage did high ground give people?
