Word Roots
Latin Root: flor- — "flower"

The Latin root flor- means "flower." And here's a fact a lot of Floridians don't know: the name Florida comes from this root. When Spanish explorer Juan Ponce de León first arrived in 1513, the land was full of flowers and bright spring blooms — so he called it La Florida, meaning "the flowered place."

🔎 Discovery Fact
The Spanish word flor still means "flower" today. And when Juan Ponce de León named Florida, he also named it during the Spanish flower-festival season — Pascua Florida — which made the name fit twice over.

Learn the Story

Florida's Own Rulebook — and Its Symbols
A state-sized version of "We the People"

The United States has a constitution for the whole country. But every state has its own constitution too. Florida's Constitution is the written rules that set up our state's government in Tallahassee. It says how laws are made, who the governor is, how courts work, and what rights Floridians have. The current Florida Constitution was rewritten in 1968 — much newer than the U.S. Constitution, but it does the same job: it puts power in the hands of the people of Florida.

Around the constitution in the case sit Florida's official symbols. A symbol is something simple — a tree, a bird, a flower, a picture — that stands for something bigger. Florida picked its symbols on purpose to honor real things from our land and our history.

🌴 State tree — the sabal palm. The sabal palm grows wild across Florida — in the wetlands, by the beaches, and along the highways. It survives hurricanes, salt spray, and hot sun. Florida picked it because it tells the truth about our land: tough, green, and everywhere.

🐦 State bird — the mockingbird. The mockingbird sings other birds' songs and adds its own twists. Floridians chose it because the mockingbird is found all over the state and is known for its bold, creative singing.

🍊 State flower — the orange blossom. The small white blossom of the orange tree. It smells sweet, and it's the start of every orange we grow. Florida grows more oranges than almost any other place in the United States.

🏞️ State seal. The state seal is a circle stamp used on official Florida papers. Inside the circle, the design shows a sabal palm tree, a sunshine, a Seminole woman, and a steamboat on the water. Together, they say: this is a place of plants, sun, native people, and water — all the things that make Florida what it is.

Council Chamber
If Florida had to pick one symbol to put on every license plate, every postcard, and every welcome sign — should it be a natural thing (the sabal palm) or a piece of history (the state seal)?
🌴 Side A: The Sabal Palm

The palm is real, alive, and everywhere in Florida. Visitors see it the second they cross the state line. It's simple, instantly recognizable, and tells the truth about our land — warm, green, and salty. A kid in Pensacola and a kid in Key West both grow up near sabal palms.

🏞️ Side B: The State Seal

The seal carries more meaning. It honors the Seminole people who lived in Florida long before statehood, our sunshine, our sabal palm, and our history of water travel — all in one image. It tells more of Florida's story than a single tree can.

Honest curators will admit both choices have something going for them. Florida actually uses both, in different places. But in this Council Chamber, you have to pick one — and explain why. Look back at the case. In Tab 4, you'll write your own recommendation, using evidence from the lesson.

⏳

Then & Now: When Florida became a state in 1845, the first state seal looked totally different. The mockingbird wasn't picked as state bird until 1927. The orange blossom became state flower in 1909. State symbols can change — and a good state keeps asking honest questions about what it wants to honor.
