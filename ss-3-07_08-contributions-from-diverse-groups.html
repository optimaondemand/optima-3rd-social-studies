🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
The Contributions Wall honors people from MANY backgrounds — Native Americans, African Americans, Asian Americans, Latino Americans, women and men, scientists, artists, leaders, and everyday helpers. Courtesy means we name each person carefully, tell their story honestly, and never decide one person's contribution is more important than another's. Every contribution matters.

Meet Six Contributors
📖
Six People Who Shaped Their Regions — and the Country
Read each profile carefully before you match them on the wall

This wall could hold thousands of contributors — and a real museum would keep adding more. Today you'll meet just six, chosen to show the many backgrounds and regions of our country. As you read each one, ask yourself: What did this person give that nobody else could?

📜
CHEROKEE NATION · SOUTHEAST & SOUTHWEST
Sequoyah (about 1770–1843)

Sequoyah was a Cherokee silversmith who created an entire writing system for his people — 86 symbols, one for each sound in the Cherokee language. Before this, Cherokee was only spoken. Within a few years of his invention, many Cherokee people could read and write in their own language. His system is still used today.

🕊️
MARYLAND → MASSACHUSETTS & NEW YORK
Frederick Douglass (1818–1895)

Born enslaved in the Southeast, Frederick Douglass taught himself to read, then escaped to the Northeast. He became one of the most powerful speakers and writers of his time, telling the truth about slavery and arguing for freedom and equal rights for African Americans. He also fought for women's right to vote.

🏛️
OHIO · MIDWEST
Maya Lin (born 1959)

Born in Ohio to Chinese-American parents, Maya Lin was only 21 years old and still in college when her design was chosen for the Vietnam Veterans Memorial in Washington, D.C. The black granite wall lists the names of every American who died in the Vietnam War. Millions of people visit it every year to remember.

⚖️
ARIZONA · SOUTHWEST
Sandra Day O'Connor (1930–2023)

Sandra Day O'Connor grew up on a cattle ranch in Arizona. She became a lawyer, then a judge, and in 1981 she became the first woman to serve on the United States Supreme Court — the highest court in the country. She served for 24 years and helped decide many important cases.

🌽
ARIZONA & CALIFORNIA · SOUTHWEST & WEST
Cesar Chavez (1927–1993)

Born in Arizona to a Mexican-American family, Cesar Chavez worked in the fields of California from the time he was a child. He saw how hard farm workers worked and how unfairly they were treated. As an adult, he organized farm workers to ask for fair pay, safer working conditions, and respect. His work changed laws and lives.

👑
HAWAII · WEST
Queen Lili'uokalani (1838–1917)

Queen Lili'uokalani was the last queen of the Kingdom of Hawaii. She loved her people and her islands, and she worked to protect Hawaiian traditions and the rights of Native Hawaiians. She was also a gifted musician who composed many songs — including "Aloha 'Oe," which is still sung today around the world.

These are six of countless contributors. Real museum walls keep growing — and so does this one.

🔎 Discovery Fact
When Maya Lin's design for the Vietnam Veterans Memorial was chosen in 1981, the judges didn't know she was a 21-year-old college student. The names on the wall are listed in the order people died — not alphabetically — so family members can trace the wall and find their loved ones in time, not just by name.

The Big Picture
🏷️ Exhibit Label

The United States is shaped by its land — but more deeply by its people. From Cherokee inventors to Hawaiian queens, from formerly enslaved abolitionists to first-generation Chinese-American architects, from farm worker organizers to Arizona Supreme Court justices, contributors from every region and background built the country we share. Every name on this wall is a real person whose work matters today.

🦉

1. Sequoyah's biggest contribution was —
AInventing a new kind of cattle ranch
BBecoming the first Cherokee president of the United States
CCreating a writing system for the Cherokee language
Right! Sequoyah created 86 symbols, one for each sound in the Cherokee language. His system is still used today.
Think back to the profile card. What did Sequoyah invent for his people?
2. Frederick Douglass is remembered for —
ADesigning famous buildings
BSpeaking and writing against slavery and for equal rights
CInventing a new musical instrument
Yes! Douglass escaped slavery, learned to read on his own, and used his voice and writing to fight for freedom and equal rights for African Americans.
Think about the words "spoke" and "wrote." Douglass used both to fight for something important.
3. What is one big idea the whole gallery — alcoves, Listening Station, and Contributions Wall — teaches a visitor?
AThe United States is shaped by many places, voices, and people working together.
BOne region is the most important and the rest don't matter.
COnly land matters — people do not.
Exactly! Land, stories, AND people together shape every region. That's the gallery's big idea.
A good curator never says one region is more important than another, and never says people don't matter. Think about what the whole gallery, from the first alcove to this wall, has shown.
