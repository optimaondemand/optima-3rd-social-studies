Four Rules of Good Money
🎬
Teacher recording notes: Walk students through four characteristics of money, kept in plain 3rd-grade language. Lasts (durable): it doesn't fall apart fast. Easy to carry (portable): you can take it where you need it. Easy to count (divisible / countable): it comes in known amounts. Trusted (recognizable / valuable): people agree it's worth something. Use the case items: gold and silver coins last forever; bills are easy to carry; coins are easy to count; everyone in the country agrees a dollar bill is worth a dollar. Then explain why salt and shells kind of worked — and why they didn't last. Walk through the Source Study questions. 3–5 minutes.

What Makes Something Money?
Four rules a curator can use to test anything

Before there were coins and bills, people traded fish for grain, eggs for cloth, or salt for tools. Trade worked — but it was slow. You had to find someone who wanted what you had and had what you wanted. So communities started using one common thing as money. But not just anything can be money.

Over time, people noticed that good money has four characteristics. It lasts. Money should not fall apart, melt, or get eaten by mice. It is easy to carry. If you can't take it to the market, it won't help you. It is easy to count. Money should come in pieces you can add, divide, and check. It is trusted. Everyone in the community has to agree it's worth something — or no one will accept it.

Gold and silver coins passed all four tests. They lasted hundreds of years. They were small and heavy enough to feel real but light enough to carry. They could be counted one by one. And whole countries trusted them. Paper bills were a newer idea — lighter than coins, but they only worked because everyone agreed the paper stood for something real.

Some things almost worked. Cowrie shells were beautiful, easy to count, and used as money in many places — but they could chip or wash away. Salt was so valuable that Roman soldiers were sometimes paid in it — but it dissolved if it got wet, so it didn't last. Knowing the rules helps a curator see why one item became money and another quietly stopped being money.

Historical Money
🖼️ A clear, bright photograph of historical coins and/or paper money — for example, a collection of antique U.S. coins (silver dollars, copper pennies), a historical U.S. banknote, or a museum-style display of coins from different time periods. The image should show texture and detail — markings, edges, portraits, dates — so students can study the object as evidence. No close-up faces of living people; portraits printed on currency are fine and expected.

Where to find one: Smithsonian Open Access (si.edu/openaccess) — search "historical coins" or "U.S. currency"; Library of Congress Digital Collections (loc.gov) — search "coin photograph" or "historical banknote"; Wikimedia Commons (commons.wikimedia.org) — search "antique coin photograph" or "historic U.S. banknote."

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember before writing your evidence responses.

1. Which of the following is NOT one of the four characteristics of good money?
AIt lasts.
BIt is easy to carry.
CIt is the same color as the country's flag.
Right! Color doesn't matter. What matters is that money lasts, is easy to carry, is easy to count, and is trusted.
Think back to the four rules. They were about how money works, not how it looks. Which one of these isn't about how it works?
2. Why didn't a block of salt make great long-term money?
ABecause salt tastes bad.
BBecause it doesn't last — it dissolves and crumbles.
CBecause nobody needed salt.
Exactly! Salt fails the very first rule — it has to last, and salt dissolves in water.
Think about what happens to salt in the rain or in a wet bag. Which rule does that break?
3. Why does money need to be trusted?
AIf people don't believe it's worth something, no one will accept it.
BBecause trust makes coins shinier.
CBecause trust changes how heavy a bill is.
Yes! Money only works because a whole community agrees that it's worth something. Without trust, it's just metal and paper.
