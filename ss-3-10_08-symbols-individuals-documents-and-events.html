Word Roots
Latin Root: uni- — "one"

The Latin root uni- means "one." It's hiding inside the name of our country — the United States. The word united means "made into one." Fifty different states. Hundreds of millions of different people. One country. That's the whole idea, in a single root.

📖

unite — to bring people or parts together into one · united — joined together as one · union — a group joined together · unity — the quality of being one

🔎 Discovery Fact
The U.S. national motto, written on the Great Seal, is E Pluribus Unum — Latin for "Out of many, one." Look closely at a dollar bill or a penny next time. It's there.

Learn the Story
📖
Four Kinds of Evidence About a Country
Symbols · Individuals · Documents · Events

Curators have a small secret: when you're trying to tell visitors who a country is, you can't just say it. You have to show them. And there are four kinds of evidence you can use. Citizenship Hall closes with all four.

🦅 Symbols. Symbols are things that stand for something bigger. The U.S. flag stands for the whole country and its 50 states. The bald eagle stands for strength and freedom. The Statue of Liberty in New York Harbor stands for welcome — for the long line of people who came here to start over. The Liberty Bell in Philadelphia stands for the news of independence ringing out. The Great Seal on official papers carries our motto, E Pluribus Unum. Symbols show what we honor.

🧑‍💼 Individuals. Specific people who shaped the country. George Washington, the first President, who refused to act like a king. Thomas Jefferson, who wrote the Declaration of Independence. Abraham Lincoln, who led the country through the Civil War and worked to end slavery. Martin Luther King Jr., who marched and spoke for fair treatment of all Americans. Individuals show who shaped us.

📜 Documents. Pieces of paper that say what the country promises and believes. The Declaration of Independence (1776) — the country's first big "yes, we're free" statement. The U.S. Constitution (1787) — the rulebook you opened in Lesson 2. The Bill of Rights — the first ten amendments to the Constitution, which protect things like freedom of speech and the right to a fair trial. Documents show what we believe in writing.

📅 Events. Big moments that tested or changed the country. The signing of the Declaration of Independence on July 4, 1776. The Civil War (1861–1865), when the country nearly broke apart over slavery and freedom. The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom in 1963, when hundreds of thousands of Americans came together to ask their country to live up to its promises. Events show what tested us.

Here's the curator's last secret. None of the four categories tells the full story alone. The flag tells you what we honor — but it doesn't tell you who fought to defend it. The Declaration tells you what we promised — but not how often we had to fight to keep that promise. The four kinds work together, like four hands holding up the same country. E Pluribus Unum. Out of many, one.

Council Chamber
If a visitor has time to study only one kind of evidence about the United States — symbols, individuals, documents, or events — which kind would help them understand the country best, and why?
🦅 Side A: Symbols & Documents

Symbols and documents are clear, simple, and easy to study. The flag tells you what we honor; the Constitution tells you what we promise. Visitors can read them, look at them, and understand the country's biggest ideas in a few minutes — without needing to know a hundred names or dates.

🧑 Side B: Individuals & Events

Individuals and events are messy, real, and human. Washington's choices, Lincoln's struggles, Dr. King's march — these show how the country has worked, failed, and tried again. A flag can't show that. A document can't either. Only the people and the moments can.

Honest curators say both sides have something. Most museums actually keep all four. But in this Council Chamber, you have to pick one side, and explain why. In Tab 4, you'll write your final recommendation — using one specific example from this lesson as evidence.

⏳

Then & Now: In 1782, leaders chose E Pluribus Unum as the motto for the brand-new United States. They were thinking about 13 separate states becoming one country. Today, "out of many, one" still works — only now the "many" is much bigger. Same words. Same idea. Bigger country.
