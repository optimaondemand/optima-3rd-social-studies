Everything You've Learned
A quick review of all your Map Room skills

Look around the Great Map Room. Every map on these walls was broken when you first arrived. Missing titles. Wrong directions. No legends. Maps in the wrong drawers. Scales that made no sense. But over the past few weeks, you fixed all of that. Here's what you can do now:

You can name the four essential map elements — title, compass rose, scale, and legend — and explain what each one does. You know all eight directions, both cardinal and intermediate. You can tell the difference between physical, political, elevation, and population maps and pick the right one for the question. You can use a scale to figure out real distances. You know why maps and globes show the Earth differently. You can read charts, graphs, tables, and photos alongside maps. And you've even planned your own map from scratch.

That's a lot of skills. The challenge today is putting them all together.

Mapmaker's Checklist Flip Cards
Tap each card to review what each map skill does

Before the challenge, review each skill. Tap a card to flip it and see the definition.

🗺️
Title
tap to flip
The title tells you what the map shows. Without it, you don't know if you're looking at a city, a country, or the whole world.
🧭
Compass Rose
tap to flip
The compass rose shows all eight directions — north, south, east, west, and the four in between. It tells you which way is which.
📏
Scale
tap to flip
The scale lets you measure real distances on a map. One inch might equal 100 miles — so you can figure out how far it really is between two places.
🔑
Legend
tap to flip
The legend (or key) explains what colors and symbols mean. A blue line might be a river; a red dot might be a city. Without it, symbols are just mystery shapes.
📂
Map Types
tap to flip
Physical maps show land features. Political maps show borders. Elevation maps show height. Population maps show where people live. Each answers a different question.
🌍
Globe vs. Flat Map
tap to flip
A globe shows Earth's true shape but is hard to carry. A flat map is easier to use but distorts size and shape — especially near the poles.

Connect the Ideas
🏷️ Exhibit Label

Map skills work together. A title tells you what the map shows. A compass rose tells you directions. A scale tells you distance. A legend tells you what the symbols mean. And knowing map types, charts, and globes helps you choose the right tool for the question. When you can use all of these together, you can understand almost any map in the world.

Quick Self-Check
🧠

Not graded — just for you! Answer these three questions to see how much you remember.

1. A visitor wants to know how far the museum is from the airport. Which map element should they use?
AThe legend
BThe scale
CThe compass rose
Right! The scale helps readers figure out real-world distances from the map.
Think about which element helps with distance. The scale is like a ruler for the map — it tells you how far apart things really are.
2. You need to find out how high the mountains are in a region. Which map type would help the most?
APolitical map
BPopulation map
CElevation map
Exactly! Elevation maps show how high or low the land is — perfect for finding mountain heights.
Mountain height is about elevation — how high the land is above sea level. Which map type shows that?
3. What's the main difference between a globe and a flat map?
AA globe shows the Earth's real shape, but flat maps stretch or squish some areas
BA globe shows more countries than a flat map
CFlat maps are more accurate than globes
That's right! Globes show the Earth's true shape, while flat maps have distortion — some areas look bigger or smaller than they really are.
Remember the lesson on distortion — when you flatten a round surface, shapes change. The globe keeps the real shape; the flat map can't.
