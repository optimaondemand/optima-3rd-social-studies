Word Roots
Latin Root: elect- — "to choose, to pick out"

The Latin root elect- means "to choose" or "to pick out." When you have a bag of crayons and you reach in and pick the green one on purpose, you've elected a crayon. An election is just a careful, public way for a whole group of people to pick something together.

🔎 Discovery Fact
The word eclectic uses the same root. It means a person who carefully picks favorites from lots of different places — eclectic music, eclectic style, eclectic taste. Same root, same idea: choosing.

Learn the Story

A Vote, Step by Step
From "We the People" to a real answer

You already know that the U.S. Constitution says power comes from the people. Voting is how the people actually use that power. A vote is one person's choice, made on purpose, that gets counted together with everyone else's choices to make a decision.

An election is the bigger event where lots of people vote at the same time. We hold elections to pick leaders (like a mayor, governor, or President) and sometimes to decide important questions (like whether a town should build a new park).

Here's how a regular election day goes in the United States, step by step:

1. Register. Before voting, adults sign up so the government knows they're a citizen and where they live. This is called registering to vote. You only have to do this part once.

2. Go to the polling place. On election day, voters go to a building near their home — often a school, library, or community center. They give their name to a worker who checks the list of registered voters.

3. Get a ballot. A ballot is the paper (or screen) that lists the choices. Each voter gets their own.

4. Vote in private. Voters step into a booth like the one in our exhibit, close the curtain, and mark their choice without anyone watching. This is called a secret ballot. Your vote is your own business.

5. Drop the ballot in the box (or machine). Then the ballots are counted carefully. The choice with the most votes — the majority — wins.

Three rules keep an election fair: every adult citizen gets one vote, votes are secret, and the counting is checked by more than one person. When all three are true, the results really do belong to "the people."

🤔

Pause & Think: Imagine your class is voting on what game to play at recess. Why would it matter if everyone could see who voted for what? What might happen?

Then & Now: In 1787, only some adult men could vote in the United States. Over time, the country changed its rules to let more people vote — Black men in 1870, women in 1920, and 18-year-olds in 1971. The word citizen kept growing. Today, almost every adult U.S. citizen has the right to vote, no matter who they are.
