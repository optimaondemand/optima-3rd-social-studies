🏛️ Virtue Spotlight — Courtesy
Courtesy in a museum means using a culture's real name — not a lazy or made-up one — and getting the facts right. The Haudenosaunee are not the same as the Maya. Spanish settlers are not the same as French settlers. Curators learn the differences so visitors can too.

Learn the Story
📖
Five Cultures of the Region
A respectful look at who came here, and what they brought

North America and the Caribbean has been home to many peoples — far too many to fit in one lesson. Today we'll meet five cultural groups that shaped this region, and that are still here today.

🪶 The Haudenosaunee (HOH-dee-no-SHOH-nee) — also called the Iroquois — are Indigenous peoples of the Northeast Woodlands. They have lived in what is now New York State, Quebec, and Ontario for over 1,000 years. They built long wooden homes called longhouses, played the game of lacrosse, and farmed three crops together that grow well as a team: corn, beans, and squash, called the "Three Sisters."

🗿 The Maya are Indigenous peoples of southern Mexico and Central America. Over 1,000 years ago, they built huge stone cities with stepped pyramids — like the one we visited at Chichén Itzá. The Maya invented the number zero in math, used a complex calendar, and were the first people in the world to make chocolate (cacao). Millions of Maya people still live in Mexico, Guatemala, and Belize today.

⛪ Spanish settlers arrived in this region starting in the late 1400s. They brought the Spanish language and the Catholic faith. They built mission churches across what is now Mexico, the U.S. Southwest, Florida, and the Caribbean. Today, Spanish is one of the most-spoken languages in this region.

⚜️ French settlers arrived in the 1600s. They settled along the St. Lawrence River in what became Quebec, Canada, and along the Mississippi River in what became Louisiana. They brought the French language. Today, Quebec is mostly French-speaking, and you can still hear French words in Louisiana place names.

🥁 African peoples were brought to this region beginning in the 1500s. Most arrived against their will, taken from West and Central Africa as part of the slave trade. Even under terrible conditions, they kept their cultures alive — passing down music, foods, farming knowledge, and stories. American jazz, blues, and gospel music all grew from African musical traditions. Today, African American culture is a deep part of life across the United States and the Caribbean.

This is not the whole list. Many other peoples — the Inuit in the Arctic, the Taíno in the Caribbean, English settlers, Chinese and Italian and many more recent arrivals — have all helped shape this region. Curators tell as much of the story as they can, respectfully.

🤔

Pause & Think: If you only learned about ONE of these cultures, you'd miss most of the story of the region. Why does a good curator make sure visitors meet many cultures, not just one?

Match the Cases
🏺
Map Quest: Match the Contribution to the Culture
Tap each item, then tap the culture it came from.

The contributions got mixed up between the cases. Read each item carefully and tap the cultural group it belongs to. Each one locks green when you match it right.

Chocolate (cacao) — the world's first chocolate, made from cacao beans grown in the warm forests of southern Mexico over 1,000 years ago.
The Three Sisters — the farming method of growing corn, beans, and squash together so they help each other grow. Used by Indigenous nations of the Northeast Woodlands.
Mission churches — small church buildings built across Mexico, Florida, the U.S. Southwest, and the Caribbean, starting in the 1500s.
The French language spoken in Quebec — still the main language of most people in the province of Quebec, Canada.
Jazz and blues music — musical styles that grew up in the southern United States and the Caribbean, rooted in African musical traditions.
Stone stepped pyramids — like the great pyramid at Chichén Itzá in Mexico's Yucatán, built over 1,000 years ago.
🎉 Cases filled!
🔎 Discovery Fact: The Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace — a kind of constitution written about 600 years ago — is one of the oldest still-living democratic governments in the world. Some historians believe it shaped ideas the writers of the U.S. Constitution used hundreds of years later.
⏳

Then & Now: When you eat chocolate, you're tasting something the Maya gave the world. When you hear jazz music, you're hearing the descendants of African musical traditions. When you say "rendezvous" or "barbecue" — those words came from French and Caribbean Indigenous people. The cultures in these cases didn't end long ago. They're around you every day.

🏷️ Exhibit Label

North America and the Caribbean has been home to many peoples. Indigenous nations have been here the longest and are still here today. Spanish, French, English, and other settlers brought languages, religions, and traditions. African peoples — brought against their will — shaped music, food, and farming across the region. Together, these cultures continue to make the region what it is.

🧭 Opti says…
A curator's tip: when we say "Native American" or "Indigenous," that includes hundreds of different nations — Haudenosaunee, Maya, Inuit, Cherokee, Navajo, and many more. Each has its own language, traditions, and story. Using a specific name is the most respectful thing a curator can do.
