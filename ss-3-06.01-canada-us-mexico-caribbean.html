Learn the Story
📖
Four Places, One Region
Meet the countries of North America and the Caribbean

The world is huge. To study it, geographers look at one region at a time. A region is a group of places that have something in common — they're close together, share borders, or are connected by oceans, history, or culture.

Today's region has four parts:

🇨🇦 Canada sits in the far north. It reaches all the way up to the Arctic and is the second-largest country on Earth by land area.

🇺🇸 The United States sits in the middle, sharing a long border with Canada to the north and Mexico to the south. It stretches from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean.

🇲🇽 Mexico sits south of the United States. Its land narrows the further south you go, connecting North America to Central America.

🏝️ The Caribbean is not one country — it's a group of islands in a warm sea east and south of Mexico. Places like Cuba, Jamaica, Haiti, the Dominican Republic, and Puerto Rico are part of the Caribbean.

All four sit close together on the map. That's why curators group them as one region.

🤔

Pause & Think: The Caribbean is mostly water and islands, while Canada is one huge country. Why might curators still group them together with the United States and Mexico?

Map Quest: Place the Labels
Match each country label to the right clue.

Read each clue and tap the country that matches it. Get all four right to finish the map.

Clue 1: "I'm the country in the far north. I'm the second-largest country on Earth and I reach all the way to the Arctic."
Clue 2: "I sit in the middle, between Canada and Mexico. I touch both the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans."
Clue 3: "I sit south of the United States and connect North America to Central America. My capital is Mexico City."
Clue 4: "We're not one country — we're a chain of islands in a warm sea. Cuba, Jamaica, and Puerto Rico are part of us."
🎉 All four labels placed!
🔎 Discovery Fact: The Caribbean Sea has more than 7,000 islands, islets, reefs, and cays. Only about 13 of them are independent countries — the rest are smaller territories or uninhabited.
⏳

Then & Now: In the past, the borders between these countries looked very different — Mexico once reached much farther north than it does now. Today, the borders we see on the map have been the same for a long time. What stayed the same? People still live close together in this region and trade across borders every day.

🧭 Opti says…
Quick curator tip: when someone says "America," they usually mean the United States — but the word "America" actually covers everything from Canada down to South America. That's why careful curators say "the United States" when they mean just one country.
