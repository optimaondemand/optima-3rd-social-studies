Curators serve museum visitors by making exhibits accurate and clear. When you ask careful questions before writing a label, you're serving the people who will read it. Rushing leads to mistakes — patience is a form of service.

Learn the Story
📖
The Five Curator Questions
From curiosity to disciplined inquiry.

Last time, you learned to sort primary and secondary sources. Now you know what kind of source you're looking at. But that's just the beginning. Before a curator writes an exhibit label, they ask five types of questions:

👁️ 1. Observation: "What do I see?" — Describe every detail you notice. Don't explain yet — just look carefully.

📍 2. Context: "When and where did this happen?" — Place the object or source in time and space. Look for dates, places, and clues.

🔍 3. Evidence: "How do I know this is true?" — Check whether the information comes from a reliable source. Is there proof?

🔗 4. Connection: "How does this connect to what I already know?" — Link new information to something you've learned before.

⚖️ 5. Significance: "Why does this matter?" — Explain why this object, event, or idea is important for the exhibit.

A curator who asks all five questions writes labels visitors can trust. A curator who skips them might put wrong information on the museum wall.
